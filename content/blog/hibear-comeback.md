Let me give you the honest version of the Hibear story, because the polished version doesn't help anyone.

We designed an insulated bottle that won a Red Dot Award. That's one of the most recognized design awards in the world. We also got an iF Design Award. The product was genuinely good. We went on Shark Tank and pitched to five sharks on national television.

We didn't get a deal.

And then — like a lot of companies that burn too bright too fast — things got hard. Supply chain problems. Cash flow crunches. The market got crowded. Stanley happened to the whole industry in a way nobody predicted.

For a lot of people, that's where the story ends. The startup that almost made it.

I'm not interested in that ending.

**What rebuilding actually looks like**

It doesn't look like a relaunch campaign. It doesn't look like a pivot deck. It looks like sitting down with a spreadsheet at 10pm after the kids are asleep and figuring out which SKUs are worth keeping, which suppliers you can still trust, and which customers actually love the product enough to come back.

It looks like being honest with yourself about what broke and why. Not the narrative you'd tell a journalist — the real version. The decisions you'd make differently. The things you optimized for that turned out to be the wrong things.

For Hibear, the answer came back to the product. The bottle itself is excellent. The design holds up. What didn't work was how we built the business around it.

**The rebuild is the interesting part**

I know that sounds like something you'd put on a motivational poster. I mean something more specific.

When you've already built something once, you have a set of hard-earned lessons that most first-time founders have to pay full price for. You know what a bad supplier relationship looks like before it blows up. You know which marketing channels are expensive and slow. You know how fast inventory can become a liability.

That knowledge is worth something. The question is whether you have the discipline to actually use it.

That's what I'm documenting on Finding Rad. Not the comeback story, because we're still in the middle of it. The actual process of building something twice, better, with less margin for error.

No guarantees. Just the work.
