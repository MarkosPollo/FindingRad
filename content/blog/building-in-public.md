Most founders spend serious energy making things look cleaner than they are. Polished decks. Curated LinkedIn posts. Announcements timed to land when the wind is at their back.

I get it. There's real risk in showing the mess. Customers might walk. Investors might pass. Competitors might copy your stumbles.

But I've found the opposite to be true.

When I started documenting the Hibear rebuild on camera, I figured a few dozen people might watch. What I didn't expect was how fast those people would become the most engaged, most loyal audience I've ever built. Not despite the rawness — because of it.

Here's what I think is actually happening.

**Trust is built in the messy middle, not at the finish line.**

Anyone can post a win. Your launch day numbers, your press hit, your award. That stuff is easy to celebrate and hard to connect with, because nobody believes it was easy to get there. They know the story is incomplete.

When you show the struggle — the failed prototype, the supplier who ghosted you, the campaign that bombed — you're doing something rare. You're being honest. And honest is magnetic right now, because most of the internet is performing.

**The right people self-select.**

When I talk about Navy Reserve weekends eating into my build schedule, or the reality of running a consumer product company with three kids, some people tune out. Good. Those aren't my people anyway.

The ones who stay are the ones who are in it too. Building something real, under real constraints, with real stakes. Those people become customers, collaborators, and critics who actually make the work better.

**You think clearer when you narrate.**

This is the one I didn't see coming. The act of explaining what I'm doing on camera — what decision I made, why, what I'd do differently — forces clarity I wouldn't get otherwise. It's like having a thinking partner who never gets tired and never bills by the hour.

Showing the mess isn't a marketing strategy. It's a discipline. And it's the one that's worked best for me.

That's the whole idea behind Finding Rad. Not a highlight reel. The actual build.
