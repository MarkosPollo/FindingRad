Parenting doesn't have a reset button. Or so most people think.

Nikki and Devon have been living full-time on a converted school bus with their two daughters — ages 10 and 3 — for five years. They've fixed breakdowns at altitude, rewired electrical systems from scratch, and spent years figuring out how two adults and two kids can share a space the size of a large bathroom without losing their minds.

Their answer? When things get hard, become a kid.

## The Dance Party Reset

It sounds too simple. It isn't.

> "When parenting gets too hard — become a kid. We wake up dancing."

Nikki and Devon don't reach for a parenting book when the wheels come off. They reach for the speaker. An impromptu family dance party is their hard reset — it works on the kids, it works on them, and it works on the relationship.

**Why it actually works:**

- It breaks the tension loop instantly
- It reminds everyone (including the adults) that joy is available right now
- It models emotional regulation by doing something instead of stewing
- It's genuinely fun, which is something adults forget to prioritize

They were friends for 10 years before they got together. The dance thing was always just part of who they were. It didn't come from a book. It came from knowing each other deeply enough to be silly without self-consciousness.

## What Bus Life Actually Teaches You

Five years on a bus isn't a vacation. It's a masterclass in constraint-driven creativity.

**The real lessons from the road:**

- **Shared survival > shared experiences.** Fixing a blown alternator at 8,000 feet together builds something that a weekend trip doesn't.
- **Small spaces force presence.** You can't disappear into different rooms. You deal with things.
- **Kids are more adaptable than you think.** Their daughters treat the country like their backyard, because it is.
- **Frustration is a trigger, not a stopping point.** When something goes wrong, they treat it as the signal to move — not shut down.

---

Most people build a life around stability and then wonder why it feels small. Nikki and Devon built theirs around motion and found that everything they needed came with them.

Freedom isn't a destination. It's a decision you make every morning — even when, especially when, you wake up dancing.
